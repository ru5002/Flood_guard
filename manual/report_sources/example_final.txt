Abstract  
 
Sri Lanka, ranked as the second most disaster-affected country globally, faces systemic challenges in 
disaster management, including fragmented tools, delayed information dissemination, and inefficient 
resource allocation, as evidenced during the 2004 tsunami and subsequent disasters. Info Unity Response 
addresses these critical gaps through a comprehensive Progressive Web Application (PWA) that integrates 
real-time disaster mapping, crowdsourced reporting, AI-driven guidance, and volunteer coordination into a 
unified platform. The application leverages modern web technologies including Vite PWA React.js, 
Firebase (Firestore, Cloud Functions, Authentication), and Open Maps to deliver a scalable solution with 
offline functionality, multilingual support (Sinhala, Tamil, English), and role-based security.   
Key features include: (1) dynamic disaster mapping combining official and crowdsourced data; (2) Quick 
disaster warning dissemination (3) a volunteer management system with skill-based matching;(4) Missing 
person registry with uploads and reunification protocols (5) Preparedness catalogues (6) an AI chatbot 
providing localized emergency instructions; (7) predictive analytics (TensorFlow.js) for resource allocation; 
and (8) geo-targeted push notifications. Stakeholder surveys confirmed high demand for real-time alerts 
(100% preference) and interactive maps (84%), while interviews with Red Cross volunteers highlighted the 
need for centralized coordination. The system aligns with SDGs 11, 3, and 17 by enhancing community 
resilience, health outcomes, and multi-stakeholder collaboration.   
Developed iteratively using Agile methodology, the project overcame challenges such as SMS gateway 
limitations and client-side machine learning. The project addresses research gaps identified in existing 
systems (e.g., FEMA, Red Cross apps), such as fragmentation, lack of real-time integration, and offline 
limitations. Current deployment [infounity-response.web.app] demonstrates 99.99% uptime during 
simulated disasters, with future work focusing on advanced offline synchronization. By digitizing Sri 
Lanka’s disaster response workflow, Info Unity Response establishes a replicable model for real-time, data-
driven crisis management in resource-constrained environments.   
 
Keywords: disaster management, real-time crowdsourcing, PWA, AI chatbot, resource prediction, SDGs, 
volunteer coordination, multilingual support.

1.Introduction 
1.1 Context 
Sri Lanka, an island nation known for its geographical diversity, is unfortunately highly susceptible to a 
wide range of natural hazards, including floods, landslides, and tsunamis. Its vulnerability is underscored by 
its ranking as the second most disaster-affected country globally according to the Global Climate Risk Index 
2017 (Eckstein, Hutfils and Winges, 2019). This persistent threat necessitates robust infrastructure and 
efficient systems for disaster preparedness and response. However, the nation faces significant challenges in 
its current disaster management framework. Past events, such as the devastating 2004 tsunami and the 2017 
floods and landslides which impacted over 630,000 people, have exposed critical weaknesses. These include 
fragmented communication tools, delays in disseminating timely and accurate information to the public, 
inefficiencies in resource allocation, and inadequate coordination among stakeholders, including volunteers. 
Often, crucial information relies on conventional media or police announcements, leading to delays and 
sometimes inaccuracies, while volunteer efforts remain largely uncoordinated.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
1.2 Project Overview  
To address these systemic gaps, the "Info Unity Response" project introduces a comprehensive disaster 
management solution. Designed as a Progressive Web Application (PWA), Info Unity Response aims to 
unify disparate functionalities into a single, accessible platform. It integrates real-time mapping of affected 
areas using official and crowdsourced data, AI-driven guidance for emergency situations, and robust 
multilingual support (Sinhala, Tamil, and English) to ensure accessibility across diverse communities. 
Info Unity Response incorporates several cutting-edge features to enhance disaster preparedness and 
response capabilities. A key component is an AI-powered chatbot, utilizing the Gemini model for better 
understanding and context but trained with disaster prompts for accuracy, because of which what it provides 
is instant, localized emergency instructions and guidance in Sinhala, Tamil, and English. The platform

application. By integrating these features, Info Unity Response seeks to digitize and significantly improve 
Sri Lanka's disaster response workflow, fostering greater resilience and more effective crisis management. 
2. Background, Objectives & Deliverables 
2.1 Project Background 
The impetus for the "Info Unity Response" project stems directly from Sri Lanka's significant vulnerability 
to natural disasters and the observed shortcomings in managing their aftermath. Historical events serve as 
stark reminders of the critical need for improved disaster response mechanisms. The 2004 Indian Ocean 
tsunami profoundly impacted the nation, and surveys conducted in its wake revealed that a staggering 94% 
of residents in affected areas had no prior knowledge of tsunamis, which severely exacerbated the impact. 
This highlighted a fundamental gap in public awareness and preparedness. 
More recently, the May 2017 floods and landslides, which affected a staggering number of people and 
caused widespread destruction, particularly in the Ratnapura, Kalutara, and Matara Districts, further exposed 
the inadequacies of the existing disaster management system. Despite efforts to establish management 
frameworks, significant challenges persist, including poor coordination among stakeholders, limited 
community-level awareness, delays in information dissemination, and inefficiencies in resource allocation 
and volunteer management.

2.2.3 Performance Objectives (Critical Success Factors (CSFs)) 
1. System Reliability – Maintain 99.99% uptime during disaster events 
Key measures: 
- Continuous server monitoring using Firebase performance monitoring and system 
tracking  
- Currently using Firebase Hosting for real-time failover capabilities 
- Usage of Cloud Firestore for reliable, distributed data storage to ensure scalability and 
availability. (Gudimetla, 2020) 
- Use of Vite React for faster builds and hot module replacement and also it has service 
workers to minimize update time for both offline and online applications 
 
2. Response Time Optimization  
Key measures: 
- Low latency data fetching using closer servers can clean optimal data pulling 
- Caching using Indexed DB and Service Workers 
- Usage of Firebase cloud functions that reduce backend operations and reduce processing 
tasks  
 
3. User Concurrency Support  
Key measures: 
- Firebase’s globally distributed content delivery network (CDN) to handle higher user 
loads. 
- Dynamic firebase database build for high concurrency environments 
- Leveraging PWA's lightweight architecture to reduce server load since it’s mostly 
extensive client-side processing. 
 
4. Offline Functionality – Ensure optimal availability offline 
Key measures: 
- Service workers to cache essential resources and ensure offline access to the most critical 
features of our system 
- Local data storage by making use of Indexed DB 
- Seamless app like experience even when offline

2.3 Project Deliverables 
The successful completion of the Info Unity Response project will result in the following key deliverables: 
• Live Progressive Web Application (PWA): A fully functional, responsive web application 
accessible at infounity-response.web.app. The PWA provides the core user interface, offline 
capabilities, and cross-device compatibility.  
 
• Realtime Geolocation based push notifications: Info Unity Response's geolocation-based push 
notification system delivers targeted emergency alerts based off the accounts location setup in real-
time so that all notification stay relevant to the user and easy to digest. 
 
• AI-Powered Chatbot: An integrated chatbot using Google's Gemini AI model, providing real-time, 
multilingual emergency guidance and answering user queries related to disaster situations.    
 
Figure 4 Google AI Studio Trained Model 
 
• Firebase-Hosted Backend Infrastructure: A complete backend system hosted on Firebase, 
encompassing: 
o Cloud Firestore and Realtime Database for data storage (disaster reports, user profiles, 
volunteer data, warnings, etc.).    
o Firebase Authentication for secure user sign-up and login.    
o Cloud Functions for serverless logic (e.g., volunteer matching, SMS processing, notification 
triggers).    
o Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) for delivering geo-targeted push notifications.    
o Firebase Hosting for deploying and serving the PWA.

• Tensor Flow powered resource prediction Model: The resource prediction algorithm estimates 
disaster relief needs by using a TensorFlow.js machine learning model trained on historical data of 
affected people, families, and infrastructure damage from Firestore to predict the required amounts of 
ten essential resources. If insufficient training data exists (less than 10 records), the algorithm 
employs a manual calculation based on fixed daily ratios per person for dry rations (water, rice, dhal, 
canned fish, milk powder, sugar, tea, biscuits, soap, toothpaste) or prepared meals (water, food 
portions, soap, toothpaste). Finally, the predicted resource quantities are rounded to practical units 
for distribution. 
 
• Offshore SMS Gateway Mechanism: A SMS gateway application that will receive all received 
SMS messages decode it with the formatting and sends it to the current system as a JSON Payload 
using a Web Hook Service.

3 Literature Review   
Several technological solutions have been proposed and implemented for disaster preparedness, response, 
and recovery in the past few years, but there are still significant gaps with respect to meeting holistic needs 
of the communities involved in terms of real-time responsiveness, multi-stakeholder collaboration, and user 
access in low-resource environments. While isolated aspects, such as emergency alerts or maps, have been 
addressed by numerous applications and systems, there is still dire a need for a multi-featured integrated 
platform that can consolidate various functionalities, hence streamlining both operational aspects such as 
volunteer overlooking or rations management along with community-level disaster responses. 
The current literature on disaster response applications indeed reflects the potential of bringing the well-
known business process management techniques into disaster response management. Existing research, such 
as the study by (Hofmann, Betke and Sackmann, 2015) develop process-oriented approaches toward 
emergency situations. However, these contributions tend to target purely structural issues and fail to 
incorporate real-time data, community involvement, or dynamic mapping which are also seen as major 
information that should be made accessible in such situations. This is what Info Unity Response is set to 
bring a minimalistic suite of tools to streamline the various processes essential for disaster response, as 
stated in the paper under process design to bring Disaster Response models for the various disasters , under

4.Method of Approach  
The development of the "Info Unity Response" application followed a structured approach encompassing an 
agile methodology, strategic tool selection, continuous stakeholder engagement, and careful consideration of 
ethical implications. 
4.1 Development Methodology 
The project adopted the Agile Development methodology, recognizing the feature-centric nature of the 
application and the need for flexibility in responding to evolving requirements. Development was organized 
into iterative cycles, specifically 2-week sprints, allowing for incremental progress and regular reviews. This 
approach facilitated the building of application components part-by-part, ensuring that core functionalities 
could be tested and refined before moving to more advanced features. Progress was tracked against an initial 
project plan, visualized in a Gantt chart, which outlined key phases from planning and design to 
implementation, testing, and final deployment over an approximate 6-month duration. This iterative process 
allowed for adjustments based on feedback and technical challenges encountered during development.    
4.2 Tools and Technologies 
A modern technology stack was selected to meet the project's requirements for performance, scalability, 
offline capabilities, and real-time functionality: 
• Frontend: Vite React was chosen as the primary framework for building a dynamic, responsive user 
interface. Its suitability for Progressive Web Applications (PWA) was leveraged, utilizing vite-
plugin-pwa and service workers to enable critical offline functionality. Tailwind CSS was used for 
efficient and responsive UI design. Multilingual support (Sinhala, Tamil, English) was implemented 
using the i18next library.    
• Backend: The Firebase suite served as the core backend infrastructure. This included Firebase 
Hosting for deployment, Cloud Firestore for flexible NoSQL data storage, Firebase Authentication 
for user management, Cloud Functions for serverless backend logic (e.g., processing SMS reports, 
volunteer matching), and Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) for push notifications.    
• Mapping: Open Maps to provide interactive, real-time mapping capabilities for visualizing disaster 
zones and resources.    
• Machine Learning: TensorFlow.js was utilized for client-side machine learning to enable resource 
prediction based on disaster impact data.  
• Artificial Intelligence Tuned Chatbot Model: Google's AI (Gemini 1.5 Flash model) powered the 
AI chatbot functionality further finetuned using 100+ example prompts used during disaster 
situations. 
• GitHub & GitHub Actions: For continuous version control and management of growing codebase 
and action in order to automate all workflows using CI/CD so that upon release in the busy agile 
environment releases are sent live back-to-back with no hassle.

7.Development & Testing  
This section details the core algorithms powering Info Unity Response, outlines the key technical features 
implemented using a top-down logic approach, summarizes the testing methodologies employed, and 
discusses the challenges encountered during development. 
7.1 Key Development Features and Algorithms 
The Info Unity Response platform incorporates several sophisticated features built upon specific algorithms 
and implementation strategies: 
1. Resource Prediction (TensorFlow.js + Manual Fallback) 
• Core Logic: The system is designed to predict the essential relief resources (like food, water, 
medical supplies) needed during a disaster, based on real-time impact data (number of affected 
people/families, infrastructure damage) collected via disaster reports. The primary prediction 
mechanism utilizes a client-side machine learning model (TensorFlow.js) trained on historical 
resource allocation data. A manual calculation method serves as a fallback when insufficient training 
data (<10 historical cases) is available in Firestore.    
• Implementation Approach:  
o A sequential neural network model (TensorFlow.js) with dense layers processes normalized 
disaster input factors.    
o The model predicts quantities for key resources (Water, Rice, Dhal, Canned fish, Milk 
powder, Sugar, Tea, Biscuits, Soap, Toothpaste). Predicted outputs are rounded to practical 
units (e.g., Liters, kg).    
o The fallback algorithm uses pre-defined, configurable ratios (e.g., 2L water/person/day, 0.5kg 
rice/person/day) which can be adjusted based on disaster type and duration.    
2. SMS Disaster Reporting and Validation 
• Core Logic: Enables users, particularly those with limited internet access, to report disaster incidents 
via SMS using a predefined format. The system automatically validates the incoming message's 
structure and content before integrating the report into the main platform.    
• Implementation Approach:  
o A Firebase Cloud Function acts as a webhook, receiving SMS messages forwarded by a 
third-party gateway.    
o Messages must adhere to the standard format: 
DISASTER#DISTRICT#DSDIVISION#TYPE#DESCRIPTION#NAME#ID.  
o The function validates the format, checks the disaster type against a predefined list, and 
ensures all required fields are present.    


--- PAGE 48 ---
 
 
48 
 
o Valid reports are stored in the crowdsourcedReports collection in Firestore with a "pending" 
status, and a reference ID is sent back to the reporter via SMS. Invalid reports trigger an error 
message response.    
3. Gemini Powered Multilingual Chatbot 
• Core Logic: Provides an AI-driven assistant accessible within the application to offer immediate 
disaster response guidance and answer user queries. It leverages Google's Gemini model for 
advanced natural language understanding and response generation in English, Sinhala, and Tamil. 
The chatbot aims to maintain conversational context for more natural interactions.    
• Implementation Approach:  
o A chat interface built with React allows users to select their preferred language.    
o Integration with the Gemini API uses tailored system prompts defining the AI's role as a 
disaster response expert and providing context based on known disaster protocols.    
o Language-specific templates ensure culturally appropriate responses.    
o Pre-programmed responses act as a fallback if the AI service is temporarily unavailable. 
4. Dynamic Login and Role-Based Authentication (RBAC) 
• Core Logic: Implements a secure authentication system that controls user access to different features 
and data based on assigned roles. Defined user types include public users, field 
responders/volunteers, administrators (e.g., DMC officials), and potentially government oversight 
roles.    
• Implementation Approach:  
o Firebase Authentication handles core user sign-up, login, and session management.    
o Custom claims within Firebase Authentication tokens are used to manage and enforce user 
roles. 
o The React frontend dynamically renders UI components, dashboards, and functionalities 
based on the authenticated user's role. 
o A progressive permission structure is used, where higher-level roles typically inherit the 
capabilities of lower-level roles. 
5. Offline-Aware Containers and Network Status Management 
• Core Logic: Ensures the application is aware of the current network connectivity status and adapts 
gracefully. Users are notified of changes in connectivity, and functionality is adjusted based on 
whether the app is online or offline. 
• Implementation Approach:  


--- PAGE 49 ---
 
 
49 
 
o React Higher-Order Components (HOCs) or Context API wrap key parts of the application to 
provide network status information. 
o Browser online/offline events are monitored to detect connectivity changes in real-time. 
o The system checks if content is being served from the cache to inform offline availability 
status. 
o User-friendly notifications (e.g., snackbars or banners) indicate the current connection status. 
o UI elements or features requiring online access may be disabled or hidden when offline. 
6. IndexedDB for Offline Data Management 
• Core Logic: Provides robust local storage capabilities, allowing users to create disaster reports, 
potentially attach files, and access cached information (like preparedness guides) even when offline. 
Data queued offline is synchronized with the backend (Firestore) once connectivity is restored.    
• Implementation Approach:  
o The Dexie.js library is used as a wrapper to simplify interactions with the browser's 
IndexedDB API.    
o Multiple IndexedDB stores are defined for different data types:  
▪ disasterReports: Stores user-submitted reports created while offline. 
▪ offlineUploads: Manages file attachments associated with offline reports. 
▪ catalogDB: Caches disaster preparedness articles and guidelines for offline reading. 
o A queue-based system manages the synchronization of offline data, potentially including 
basic conflict resolution logic. 
o Background sync capabilities via the service worker are leveraged where browser support 
allows. 
7. i18next Translations 
• Core Logic: Ensures the entire application interface is available in Sri Lanka's official languages 
(Sinhala, Tamil) as well as English. Users can dynamically switch languages without needing to 
reload the application.    
• Implementation Approach:  
o The i18next framework and its React integration (react-i18next) are used for managing 
translations.    
o Translation strings are organized into namespaced JSON files, typically categorized by 
application feature or component.

50 
 
o Language detection can be based on browser settings initially, with user preference 
overriding it. 
o Appropriate handling for Right-to-Left (RTL) text rendering is included where necessary 
(e.g., for Tamil script elements). 
 
8. Advanced Service Worker Configuration 
• Core Logic: Provides the foundation for the PWA's offline capabilities, intelligent caching, and push 
notification handling.    
• Implementation Approach:  
o Workbox is used to generate and manage the service worker logic. 
o Multiple caching strategies are employed:  
▪ Network-first: For frequently updated dynamic content like disaster status or real-time 
map data. 
▪ Cache-first: For static assets like core application scripts, CSS, and images. 
▪ Stale-while-revalidate: For semi-dynamic content like forms or catalogue articles, 
ensuring quick loads while updating in the background. 
o Route-specific handlers are defined to apply the appropriate caching strategy based on the 
request URL or type. 
o Background sync is configured to handle the submission of queued offline reports.    
o Integration with Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) enables the service worker to receive and 
display push notifications even when the app is not actively open.    
o Cache management policies (e.g., maximum age or number of entries) are set to prevent 
excessive storage usage.

9 Project Post-Mortem 
Reflecting on the development lifecycle of the "Info Unity Response" project provides valuable insights into 
successful strategies, areas requiring refinement, and key takeaways for future endeavours. 
Successes 
Several factors contributed significantly to the project's progress and the successful delivery of core 
functionalities: 
1. Agile Methodology Flexibility: The adoption of the Agile development methodology, specifically 
using 2-week sprints, proved highly effective. It allowed the development process to remain flexible 
and adaptive to emerging challenges and changing priorities. This iterative approach facilitated 
incremental progress, regular testing, and the ability to incorporate feedback throughout the 
development cycle, ensuring the final product was more aligned with evolving needs. 
2. Stakeholder-Guided Feature Prioritization: Engaging stakeholders early, particularly through 
surveys with the local community and interviews with Red Cross personnel, was a major success. 
The clear data gathered from surveys (e.g., the high demand for real-time alerts and interactive 
maps) provided objective justification for prioritizing certain features. This ensured that development 
efforts were focused on functionalities that offered the most value to end-users and addressed 
validated needs within the Sri Lankan context. 
Areas for Improvement 
While the project achieved many of its goals, certain aspects could have been managed differently for 
potentially smoother execution: 
1. Earlier and More Persistent DMC Engagement: Although attempts were made to collaborate with 
the Disaster Management Centre (DMC) of Sri Lanka, securing consistent engagement proved 
challenging. Initiating formal discussions and demonstrating prototype value propositions much 
earlier in the project lifecycle might have fostered a stronger collaborative relationship. Persistent 
follow-up and potentially tailoring initial demonstrations specifically to DMC's immediate 
operational pain points could also have been more effective in securing their active participation and 
input. 
2. SMS Gateway Research and Budgeting: The challenges encountered with integrating SMS 
functionality highlighted a need for more thorough upfront research and budgeting for third-party 
services. The lack of readily available, cost-effective local SMS gateways was underestimated. A 


--- PAGE 54 ---
 
 
54 
 
more detailed investigation into available options, associated costs, reliability, and potential technical 
limitations early in the planning phase would have allowed for better budget allocation and 
potentially exploring alternative communication channels if necessary. 
 
Lessons Learned 
The project yielded important technical and strategic lessons: 
1. Balancing BaaS Dependencies vs. Customization: The extensive use of Firebase as a Backend-as-
a-Service (BaaS) platform significantly accelerated development, provided inherent scalability, and 
simplified infrastructure management. However, this reliance also brought limitations in terms of 
deep customization and potential long-term vendor lock-in. The key lesson learned is the critical 
importance of consciously evaluating the trade-offs between the speed and convenience offered by 
BaaS platforms like Firebase and the flexibility and control offered by more custom backend 
solutions. The optimal choice depends heavily on specific project requirements, long-term scalability 
plans, budget constraints, and the need for specialized functionalities not easily accommodated by 
the platform. Future projects will benefit from a more explicit analysis of these factors during the 
initial architecture design phase. 
2. Importance of Localized Testing for AI: Implementing the AI chatbot highlighted the complexities 
of localization beyond simple translation. Ensuring the AI provided culturally relevant and 
contextually accurate advice in Sinhala and Tamil required more than just integrating the API; it 
demanded significant effort in prompt engineering and testing with native speakers. This underscores 
the need to allocate specific time and resources for rigorous localized testing when deploying AI 
features in multilingual contexts. 
3. Proactive Risk Mitigation for External Services: The SMS gateway issue serves as a broader 
lesson in managing dependencies on external services. Proactive risk assessment, including 
identifying backup options and understanding the full cost implications (including potential usage 
spikes during actual disasters), is essential for critical functionalities relying on third-party providers. 
10 Conclusions 
The "Info Unity Response" project represents a significant step towards modernizing disaster management 
in Sri Lanka. By successfully developing and deploying a comprehensive Progressive Web Application 
(PWA), the project directly addresses critical shortcomings identified in the existing disaster response 
mechanisms, such as fragmented tools, delayed information dissemination, and inefficient coordination. 
10.1 Impact 
The primary impact of Info Unity Response lies in its potential to digitize and streamline Sri Lanka’s 
disaster response workflow. Integrating real-time mapping, crowdsourced reporting (with verification), AI-
driven guidance, volunteer coordination, and resource prediction into a single platform enhances situational 
awareness, facilitates quicker decision-making, and improves the efficiency of relief efforts. The platform's 
multilingual support and PWA architecture, including offline capabilities, ensure greater accessibility for 
diverse communities and resilience against infrastructure disruptions common during crises.

crisis management. By focusing on community resilience (SDG 11), supporting timely aid delivery (related 
to SDG 3 - Good Health and Well-being), and fostering multi-stakeholder collaboration (SDG 17), the 
project demonstrates how targeted technological solutions can contribute to broader sustainable 
development goals, particularly in resource-constrained environments vulnerable to climate change impacts. 
10.2 Future Work 
While the project has achieved substantial success, several areas present opportunities for future 
enhancement and development to maximize the platform's impact: 
1. Advanced Offline Synchronization: Further develop the offline capabilities, particularly focusing 
on more robust synchronization mechanisms for large datasets or complex interactions (e.g., 
collaborative map editing offline) and ensuring seamless conflict resolution when multiple users 
submit data offline. 
2. Expanded AI and Model Training and Capabilities: Continue refining the AI chatbot and 
TensorFlow model training by expanding its training data, particularly with more localized scenarios 
and dialects for Sinhala and Tamil too if possible. Explore integrating the AI more deeply into other

in data analysis.

Literature Review 
Several technological solutions have been proposed and implemented for disaster preparedness, response, 
and recovery in the past few years, but there are still significant gaps with respect to meeting holistic needs 
of the communities involved in terms of real-time responsiveness, multi-stakeholder collaboration, and user 
access in low-resource environments. While isolated aspects, such as emergency alerts or maps, have been 
addressed by numerous applications and systems, or a scenario where different organizations use varied 
tools and procedures leading to interoperability issues (Vollmer et al., 2024) , there is still dire a need for a 
multi-featured integrated platform that can consolidate various functionalities bringing into one place the 
critical features such as alerts and mapping which are the essentials (Syukron et al., 2024) , hence 
streamlining both operational aspects and our additional functions such as volunteer overlooking or rations 
management along with community-level disaster responses. 
 
The current literature on disaster response applications indeed reflects the potential of bringing the well-
known business process management techniques into disaster response management. Existing research, such 
as the study by (Hofmann, Betke and Sackmann, 2015) develop process-oriented approaches toward 
emergency situations. However, these contributions tend to target purely structural issues and fail to 


--- PAGE 74 ---
 
 
74 
 
incorporate real-time data, community involvement, or dynamic mapping which are also seen as major 
information that should be made accessible in such situations. This is what Info Unity Response is set to 
bring a minimalistic suite of tools to streamline the various processes essential for disaster response, starting 
off with a design to bring Disaster Response models for the various disasters , under which falls the attempt 
to use simple language and our plan to further it by making it multi-lingual to fit even more populations 
within the niche this application is targeted for. Under the overall process in terms of the initial step process 
configuration we plan to integrate real time info from sources possible or entry to system from targets 
collectively from parties like the DMC (Disaster Management Centre) Sri Lanka and the Sri Lanka Red 
Cross. Under process enactment allowing the information to flow through the system efficiently and filter it 
to prevent overload and ensure that the responders receive only data relevant for their tasks and knowledge. 
Then post event doing a comprehensive evaluation and analysis based off logs received through the 
mechanism since everything will be on system unlike the current context. 
 
(Gregory, 2010) then goes on to say that despite a few attempts in applying Web 2.0 technologies in the way 
it sufficed during the time of writing of that paper the methodologies used there are more generic in terms of 
the ways they used to crowdsource information based off the most mild rubrics and how those data was 
collected and stored for future use we plan to solve this by implementing the disaster response models for 
data collection which would allow entry of up to standard data which will be verified by authorities who will 
understand the extend of the disaster and publish them accordingly. This will be set to dynamically change 
the level of data collection too based off the different level of sudden events ensuring that data quality meets 
specific requirement and we get what we really want to aggregate. 
 
This points to a gap in the development of platforms that combine a clear interface for data reflection, AI 
support where necessary for the system to become dynamic, and progressive web application functionalities, 
which becomes quite central for areas where internet access might be disrupted during crises. Moreover, 
there is a high number of mobile applications-including those shown in the review by (Bachmann et al., 
2015) that provide preparedness resources and emergency alerts; however, most target very narrow user 
groups, such as health providers or emergency responders, and similarly lack multi-lingual support, or the 
main point of its accessibility which is offline access, and another feature which is a volunteer management 
framework, making them poorly usable, in particular for diverse communities characterized by dispersed 
language barriers or unstable internet connections.

That is why we planned to build on the advantages of a PWA (Progressive Web Application) technology, 
meaning that it will function offline-a very key factor during disasters when connectivity is minimal this has 
also been adopted by apps like “Because Life Matters” already now in the industry which leverage server-
based technology to maintain communication and coordination during disasters, even offline (Hanji et al., 
2023). Most solutions rely heavily on steady internet access (Gregory, 2010), which is not usually feasible in 
most disaster scenarios. A PWA fills this gap in that users are able to access essential information such as 
their emergency response plan steps when offline the paper goes on the state the fact that most of the current 
applications are also mobile applications we also are not going for this approach since people will not have a 
application that doesn’t have to be used every time just installed and taking up space on your device, we as 
developers can send updates instantly when in distress rather than having to push updates through app 
updates and since we can gain the benefits in terms of SEO and also stuff like Bandwidth Management. The 
push notification also ensures the system is effective even in a low-resource environment, an aspect where 
most, if not all, existing applications in disaster management fail. 
 
Moreover, resource mapping does not usually go beyond simple services such as medical help or shelters 
without real-time predictive analytics or crowdsourced information from the affected areas. These gaps 
indicate a need for a solution that is accessible, data-driven, and inclusive of both community and official 
response mechanisms. 
 
The proposed project will target such inadequacies through the development of a comprehensive, multi-
functional platform that integrates numerous key functionalities that run through the various spectrum of 
disaster response from warning to structured display, support features and then post feedback. 
 
It shall further provide a real-time mapping of disaster-affected areas that are dynamically updated based on 
official reports and crowdsourced data. This functionality will transcend the static mapping capabilities and 
sometimes tabular data display of the current DMC which are the forefront current solutions through the 
incorporation of proper cleaned up data collection mechanisms, understanding and display. Base machine 
learning algorithms that will be used for necessary resource allocations based on the real-time input disaster 
affect data. This system will also implement an AI-enabled chatbot that will instruct dealing with 
emergencies in several languages and shall be capable of operating beyond mere simple queries. This 
feature shall make this platform very different from the available systems, since even the relatively complex 
queries are efficiently resolved for users with limited technical knowledge and non-native speakers alike.

Additionally, this project will incorporate a robust volunteer enrolment and management system, allowing 
users to register, offer skills, and receive alerts for volunteer opportunities based on proximity and skill 
relevance. This feature will be enhanced with a comprehensive dashboard for coordinators, enabling them to 
track volunteer contributions and manage deployments, which is not adequately covered by existing 
applications or only currently exists in standalone application outside the disaster response workflow. 
(Bachmann et al., 2015) 
 
One of the major challenges in disaster management is finding the basic resources, but also estimating the 
quantity that will be needed and managing the same quantity, more so in terms of dry rations, food, and 
shelter. Most systems operating currently lack strong prediction models that estimate the quantity to be used 
during relief operations; this is a major cause of inefficiency in resource distribution, according to (Gregory, 
2010). To address this, our solution utilizes machine learning algorithm to analyse real-time disaster data, 
such as affected area size, number of families displaced and headcount displaced to forecast the specific 
amounts of dry rations, food, and shelter support needed. By leveraging business process management 
(BPM) methodologies, the system interfaces with relief coordinators on-site to manage inventory and 
distribution according to real-time needs, thus ensuring efficient resource deployment(Hofmann, Betke and 
Sackmann, 2015).Interfacing with a market price model for essentials, the system also generates a 
transparent budget estimate, presenting the public with an itemized donation goal for resource allocation to 
where it will do the most good and at the same time reduce corruption. Those needs, forecasted, will provide 
the on-site coordinators with a guide based on a DRP and ensure the correct resources are sent and 
distributed effectively. The process-driven approach applied here aligns with the business continuity plans 
used in disaster recovery, ensuring structured, data-informed decision-making. 
Therefore, against the background of such facts, although quite much has been done in the field of disaster 
response technologies, the proposed project will bridge obvious gaps by developing a multi-faceted 
integrative platform capable of supporting real-time data processing, multi-lingual support, offline 
functionality, and volunteer management. The proposed solution takes a comprehensive look at further 
streamlining the emergency response while allowing the community to participate in disaster preparedness 
and recovery actively, making it comprehensive for the response team as well as the public.

Ethical Considerations 
Data Privacy 
When it comes to data privacy info unity response has a huge responsibility since it handles a lot of personal 
information right from missing person details to location data and volunteer information. Due to this highly 
personal information, we are liable to implement robust data protection, use encryption mechanisms, strict 
access control based off the user roles and manage clear data retention policies. Apart from mechanisms we 
should also make sure that the user has consent that we maintain all these data and from our end we should 
make sure for what and how we retain this information the durations and backup mechanisms. 
 
Information Accuracy  
We are here dealing with a saving lives scenario so accurate and appropriate information in a quickly 
accessible way is very necessary. Our system should make sure it has vigorous manual ability for 


--- PAGE 85 ---
 
 
85 
 
verification through the official channels and sources. Data from crowdsourced reports undergoes validation 
by authorized personnel before publication. The system maintains frequent update cycles for critical 
information like disaster zones and resource availability and that these are also verified. Clear mechanisms 
exist for correcting misinformation, with an audit trail of changes. Users are notified of significant updates 
or corrections to ensure they have the most current information for decision-making. 
 
Access Equality  
Sri Lanka has a very diverse population with varying levels of technology, so ensuring equal access is 
critical. The system as of now is set to support multiple devices through the progressive web app 
architecture and we’ll make sure it’s not too heavy and can run on both modern and old devices and that 
they have minimal data requirement to be able to run in low bandwidth conditions .Obliviously this 
technology would help those in poor connectivity areas and keep them with the most necessary info at times 
of drop. The next crucial multilingual feature will allow those of any ethnicity to connect and have clean and 
quick access to whatever information they require.  
 
All these ethical considerations are what we understood as those that play a huge role in our disaster 
management system in order to be effective in scenarios and at the same time maintain trustworthiness in the 
community no matter the ethnicity or instance while protecting the user privacy and information integrity.

1.2 Problem Definition  
When it comes to the problem buildup side of the project, we are solving a lot this includes  solving to build 
an integrated logging and dissemination system that offers real-time information about disasters to the public. 
As observed in recent disasters, many respondents indicated that their primary source of information during 
emergencies was the national media or police announcements. This method, though accessible, often leads to 
delays and, at times, inaccurate information reaching affected communities.  Studies following the 2004 
tsunami showed that a lack of knowledge about tsunamis and other hazards contributed to the high mortality 
rates (Kurita et al., 2006). Though there have been awareness efforts regarding disasters, the public has relied 
most of the time on these conventional dissemination channels, which proved to be inconsistent or outdated 
in fast-moving disasters. (Herath et al., 2020) 
Other crucial factors include a lack of an institutional platform in Sri Lanka's system for disaster management 
that would support and engage volunteers. Although non-governmental organizations, such as the Red Cross, 
play important roles during disaster r ecoveries, there is no mechanism for the effective mobilization and 
coordination of volunteers according to location, skillset, and specific needs. Volunteers form an integral part 
of disaster response and usually play a significant role in rural and inacc essible areas where government 
resources may be slow to arrive or insufficient in number (Herath et al., 2020). Disaster response, without a 
structured system for volunteer recruitment, management, and deployment in place, is often fragmented and 
less effective. 
Furthermore, no mechanism exists in Sri Lanka for crowdsourcing data from the public to further inform real-
time decision-making in disaster situations. Crowdsourcing would permit citizens to report on local conditions 
and thus greatly enhance situational awareness for authorities in areas that are otherwise difficult or impossible 
to access. Current disaster management systems are unable to effectively incorporate community 
contributions. This prohibits the government from gaining a full understanding of t he scope and scale of a 
disaster in near-real time (Siriwardana, Jayasiri and Hettiarachchi, 2018). Another important issue is resource 
allocation and distribution in case of disasters. Reports indicate corruption and inefficiencies in the distribution 
of food, medical aid, and shelter during disasters. Without accountability through a proper system of 
transparency, unequal distribution of resources takes place, where there is excess aid received by some 
communities and shortages of such in others (Siriward ana, Jayasiri and Hettiarachchi, 2018). A model of 
resource distribution without corruption and based on assessed needs will ensure the overall effectiveness of 
disaster management. Although the disaster preparedness training programs introduced to Sri Lan ka greatly 
enhanced the skills and knowledge of local officials and communities, these have not yet been scaled up 
nationally and remain confined to specific areas. Despite the establishment of disaster management 
committees at every level and the rolling out of DRR programs, these latter often fall short in terms of long -
term commitment toward involvement with local populations. Consequently, the public remains largely 
unprepared for disasters, with limited access to resources and knowledge that could help  them respond 
effectively in real-time (Herath et al., 2020). 
The closing of these systemic gaps calls for upgrading and digitizing the disaster management framework  
(Sülog̃lu, Kalem and Yazıcı, 2024)  in Sri Lanka. Advanced mapping, crowdsourced data, and volunteer 
coordination through a centralized real -time platform can enhance the country's disaster response capability 
manifold. This platform would provide valid, timely, and locally relevant information to everybody, reducing 
losses associated with delays in the dissemination of information. This platform would als o create a very 
transparent, needs-based resource allocation system that eliminates corruption and ensures that aid reaches the

2.5 Drawbacks of Existing Systems 
Drawback 1 - Fragmentation of Tools 
Issue: Many existing systems, such as the American Red Cross apps, are disaster-specific, requiring users to 
download multiple apps for different scenarios. This fragmentation leads to inefficiencies and confusion 
during emergencies. 
Solution: Info Unity Response is a unified platform that integrates multiple disaster management features, 
including real-time mapping, volunteer coordination, and resource allocation, into a single application. 
Drawback 2 - Lack of Real-Time Data Integration: 
Issue: Systems like the FEMA app and Disaster Alert focus primarily on alerts and lack real -time data 
integration from multiple sources, such as crowdsourced reports and official updates. 
Solution: Info Unity Response integrates real -time data from both official and crowdsourced sources,

6.4 Algorithms 
1. Resource Prediction Algorithm 
The resource prediction algorithm is designed to estimate the amount of essential resources needed during a 
disaster based on the number of affected people, families, and infrastructure damage. The algorithm uses a 
combination of manual calculations and machine learning (TensorFlow.js) to predict the required resources. 
Steps in the Resource Prediction Algorithm: 
Data Collection and Preprocessing: The algorithm collects data on the number of affected people, 
families, and infrastructure damage from the disaster report form. The data is normalized and prepared for 
input into the machine learning model. 
Model Initialization: A TensorFlow.js sequential model is initialized with three dense layers: Input layer 
with 3 units (affected people, affected families, and infrastructure damage). Hidden layer with 64 units and 
ReLU activation. Output layer with 10 units (one for each resource type) and ReLU activation to ensure 
non-negative outputs. 
Model Training: The model is trained using historical data from Firestore (ResourceData collection). 
The training data includes: 
• Total displaced people. 
• Families affected. 
• Infrastructure damage. 
• Predicted and actual resource usage (e.g., dry rations, prepared meals). 
The model is compiled with the Adam optimizer and mean squared error loss function. Training is 
performed for 100 epochs with a batch size of 32. 
Resource Prediction: Once trained, the model predicts the required resources based on the input data 
(affected people, families, and infrastructure damage). 
The predicted resources include: 
• Water (Liters). 
• Rice (kg). 
• Dhal (kg). 
• Canned fish (units). 
• Milk powder (kg). 
• Sugar (kg). 
• Tea (kg). 
• Biscuits (packs). 
• Soap (bars). 
• Toothpaste (tubes). 
The predicted values are rounded to practical units (e.g., Liters, kg, units) 


--- PAGE 127 ---
 
 
127 
 
Fallback Mechanism: If there is insufficient training data (less than 10 documents), the algorithm falls back 
to a manual calculation method. The manual calculation estimates resources based on fixed ratios per person 
per day:  
Dry Rations: 
• Water: 2 Liters per person per day. 
• Rice: 0.5 kg per person per day. 
• Dhal: 0.2 kg per person per day. 
• Canned fish: 0.1 cans per person per day. 
• Milk powder: 0.05 kg per person per day. 
• Sugar: 0.05 kg per person per day. 
• Tea: 0.02 kg per person per day. 
• Biscuits: 0.1 packs per person per day. 
• Soap: 0.1 bars per person per day. 
• Toothpaste: 0.05 tubes per person per day. 
Prepared Meals: 
• Water: 2 liters per person per day. 
• Food portions: 3 meals per person per day. 
• Soap: 0.1 bars per person per day. 
• Toothpaste: 0.05 tubes per person per day. 
Resource Rounding: The predicted resources are rounded to practical values: 
• Water: Nearest litre. 
• Rice, Dhal, Milk powder, Sugar, Tea: Nearest 0.5 kg. 
• Canned fish, Biscuits, Soap, Toothpaste: Nearest whole number. 
2. Disaster Response AI Algorithm 
The disaster response AI is built using Google's Gemini AI model, which is fine-tuned to provide disaster-
related advice and responses. The AI is integrated into a chatbot that can handle queries in multiple 
languages (English, Sinhala, and Tamil). 
Steps in the Disaster Response AI Algorithm: 
Model Initialization: The Gemini AI model is initialized with a system prompt that defines its role as a 
disaster response expert. The system prompt includes a list of valid disaster types (e.g., flood, landslide, 
drought) and instructs the model to provide clear, actionable advice. 
Language Handling: The AI can handle queries in English, Sinhala, and Tamil.The system prompt is 
dynamically adjusted based on the selected language to ensure culturally and linguistically appropriate 
responses. 


--- PAGE 128 ---
 
 
128 
 
Query Processing: When a user submits a query, the AI processes the input and generates a response using 
the fine-tuned Gemini model. The response includes specific safety measures, mitigation strategies, and 
actionable advice related to the disaster type mentioned in the query. 
3. SMS Webhook Algorithm 
The SMS webhook algorithm processes disaster reports submitted via SMS. The algorithm validates the 
report format, checks the disaster type, and stores the report in Firestore. 
Steps in the SMS Webhook Algorithm: 
Message Parsing: The SMS message is parsed to extract the district, DS division, disaster type, description, 
reporter name, and ID number. 
The message must follow the format: 
DISASTER#DISTRICT#DSDIVISION#TYPE#DESCRIPTION#NAME#ID. 
Validation: The disaster type is validated against a list of valid disaster types. If the format or disaster type 
is invalid, an error message is sent back to the user. 
Data Storage: If the report is valid, it is stored in the Firestore crowdsourcedReports collection with a status 
of "pending”. A reference ID is generated and sent back to the user via SMS. 
 
4. Notification Algorithm 
The notification algorithm sends warnings to users in affected areas via Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM). 
Steps in the Notification Algorithm: 
Warning Trigger: When a new warning is created in the Firestore warnings collection, the algorithm 
triggers a notification to users in the affected DS division. 
User Filtering: The algorithm queries the user’s collection to find users in the affected DS division. It 
retrieves the FCM tokens of these users. 
Notification Sending: A multicast message is sent to all users in the affected area. The message includes the 
warning type, severity, and a custom sound. 
Token Management: If any tokens are invalid (e.g., the user has uninstalled the app), they are removed 
from the user's FCM tokens in Firestore.

Appendix C: Interviews  
Interview with Mr. Anton Victoria (Red Cross, Gampaha Branch) 
Interviewee: Mr. Anton Victoria, Chairman, Sri Lanka Red Cross Society - Gampaha Branch 
Interviewer: Jude Darren Victoria (Project Developer) 
Date: 13th September 2024 
Mode: Physical 
Purpose: To understand current disaster response practices within the Red Cross Gampaha branch, identify 
challenges and opportunities for process streamlining, and gather requirements for the "Info Unity 
Response" application, particularly concerning volunteer management and coordination. 
1. Introduction & Context: 
The interview commenced with an introduction to the "Info Unity Response" project, outlining its goal to 
create an integrated digital platform for disaster management in Sri Lanka. The specific focus on improving 
coordination, information dissemination, and resource management was highlighted. Mr. Victoria provided 
an overview of the Gampaha Red Cross branch's role during local emergencies, primarily focusing on floods 
and storm-related incidents common in the region. 
2. Current Disaster Response Practices (Gampaha Branch): 
Mr. Victoria detailed the typical workflow when a disaster strikes or is imminent within their operational 
area: 
• Alert Reception: Initial alerts often come via official channels (DMC, Police), media reports, or 
direct calls from affected community members or local Red Cross volunteers already on the ground. 
• Volunteer Mobilization: Mobilization relies heavily on existing communication channels. Key team 
leaders are contacted first, often via phone calls or dedicated WhatsApp groups. These leaders then 
cascade the information to their respective volunteer teams. Maintaining up-to-date contact lists and 
volunteer availability is a manual process. 
• Needs Assessment: Initial assessments are often conducted rapidly by the first responders or local 
volunteers who report back via phone or messages. Gathering comprehensive, accurate information 
quickly from multiple affected areas can be challenging. 
• Resource Deployment: Decisions on deploying resources (relief items, first aid teams, transport) are 
made by the branch leadership based on available information and resource stock. Coordination 
involves manual tracking of deployed assets and personnel. 
• Communication: Internal communication primarily uses phone calls and WhatsApp. 
Communication with external agencies (like the DMC or local government authorities) follows 
official protocols but can sometimes experience delays. Communicating updates to the wider 
volunteer base or affected public relies on cascading messages or public announcements. 
 


--- PAGE 147 ---
 
 
147 
 
3. Challenges and Opportunities for Streamlining: 
Mr. Victoria identified several key challenges and areas where processes could be improved: 
• Volunteer Coordination: The biggest challenge is efficiently mobilizing the right volunteers (based 
on skills like first aid, logistics, assessment) to the right location quickly. Knowing who is available, 
where they are, and what skills they possess in real-time is difficult with current manual methods. 
Delays occur in reaching everyone and confirming availability. 
• Real-time Information: Receiving timely, verified information from affected zones is crucial but 
often delayed or fragmented. Relying on phone calls can lead to bottlenecks and potential 
inaccuracies in relaying information. A centralized system for reporting and viewing verified updates 
would be highly beneficial. 
• Skill Matching: Matching volunteer skills to specific needs on the ground is often done based on 
memory or manual lists, which isn't always optimal. 
4. Potential Benefits of "Info Unity Response": 
Mr. Victoria expressed significant interest in the potential of the "Info Unity Response" application to 
address the identified challenges: 
• Improved Volunteer Management: A dedicated module for volunteer registration (including skills, 
location, availability) and automated matching/alerting based on incident needs was seen as 
extremely valuable. This could drastically reduce mobilization time and ensure better skill 
deployment. 
• Centralized Information Hub: Having a single platform for receiving verified disaster reports, 
viewing affected areas on a map, and accessing official alerts would improve situational awareness 
for branch leadership and deployed teams. 
• Enhanced Communication: Geo-targeted push notifications could be used to alert specific 
volunteer groups or even registered community members in affected areas more efficiently than 
current methods. 
• Streamlined Reporting: If the app allows volunteers to log activities or resource distribution easily, 
it could simplify post-disaster reporting and accountability. 
5. Specific Requirements and Needs from the Application: 
Based on the discussion, the following were identified as key needs from the Red Cross Gampaha branch 
perspective: 
• Robust Volunteer Module: 
o Easy registration process for volunteers (capturing contact details, location, skills, certifications, 
availability status). 
o Ability for administrators to search/filter volunteers based on skills, location, and availability.

148 
 
o Mechanism to send targeted alerts/mobilization requests to specific volunteer groups based on 
incident needs. 
o A way for volunteers to easily update their availability status. 
o (Desirable) Basic tracking of volunteer deployment hours/tasks for reporting. 
• Clear Information Display: 
o An intuitive map interface showing verified incident locations and severity. 
o Access to official alerts and warnings relevant to the Gampaha district. 
• Reliability and Accessibility: The application must be reliable, especially during high-demand 
periods, and accessible (potentially with offline capabilities for core information/reporting) as 
network connectivity can be poor during disasters. Multilingual support (Sinhala primarily for local 
volunteers) is important.

Mr. Victoria concluded that a platform like "Info Unity Response," particularly with a strong volunteer 
management component, has the potential to significantly enhance the efficiency and effectiveness of the 
Red Cross Gampaha branch's disaster response operations. He expressed willingness to explore the platform
