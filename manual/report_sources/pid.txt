Chapter 01: Introduction  
  
1.1 Background and Context  
Flooding represents one of the most devastating natural disasters affecting Sri Lanka, with the 
Gampaha District experiencing recurring catastrophic events that result in substantial loss of 
life and economic damage. The Aththanagalu Oya river basin, characterized by its flat terrain 
and rapid urbanization, remains particularly vulnerable to flood disasters. Recent events, 
including Cyclone Ditwah in late 2025, demonstrated the critical inadequacy of existing 
warning infrastructure when thousands of residents received alerts only after their homes 
were already inundated.  
  
The Cyclone Ditwah disaster of late 2025 provides stark evidence of systemic failure in 
current warning mechanisms. The cyclone brought unprecedented rainfall to southwestern Sri 
Lanka, with the Gampaha District experiencing water levels rising by over two meters within 
hours. Official reports documented 23 confirmed fatalities, with 87% of deaths occurring 
among residents who attempted evacuation after floodwaters had already reached dangerous 
levels. Over 15,000 residents required displacement to temporary shelters, while direct 
property losses exceeded LKR 3.2 billion. Post-disaster surveys revealed that approximately 
73% of affected households received no advance warning before floodwaters reached their 
properties, with the remaining receiving alerts averaging only 35 minutes is insufficient for 
meaningful protective action.  
  
Historical patterns demonstrate this is not an isolated incident. Major flooding events in 2016, 
2017, 2018, 2020, 2022, and 2024 followed similar trajectories of inadequate warning, 
insufficient response time, and preventable casualties. The cumulative economic impact over 
the past decade exceeds LKR 15 billion, representing systematic erosion of household wealth 
and community resilience.  
  
The Aththanagalu Oya basin presents unique hydrological challenges demanding localized 
predictive solutions. Unlike larger river systems, this watershed exhibits rapid flood response 
with intervals between rainfall onset and peak discharge averaging only 3-5 hours. This 
results from steep upper catchment slopes, short flow distances, and extensive impervious 
surfaces from urbanization. Research consistently demonstrates that effective flood response 
requires minimum 3-6 hours warning lead time, current systems provide merely 30-45 
minutes at best.  
  
FloodGuard bridges this critical gap by developing a comprehensive predictive system 
specifically calibrated for the Aththanagalu Oya basin's characteristics. Leveraging Long 
Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neural networks trained on historical data, the platform 
generates localized flood probability forecasts with 6-12 hour prediction windows, enabling 
sufficient time for protective action and safe evacuation.  
  
1.2 Problem Statement  
  
Current flood management approaches in Sri Lanka face three interconnected challenges that 
systematically undermine community safety and disaster preparedness. First, existing 
warning systems provide critically insufficient lead time, with residents in the Aththanagalu

Oya basin receiving notifications approximately 30-45 minutes before or after floodwaters 
reach their properties. Research in disaster management consistently demonstrates that 
effective protective action including securing property, evacuating vulnerable family 
members, and mobilizing emergency resources requires minimum warning periods of 3-6 
hours.   
  
The current 30-45 minute window allows virtually no meaningful response.  
Second, centralized monitoring by the Disaster Management Centre and Department of 
Irrigation relies on fixed water level thresholds rather than predictive analytics, creating a 
purely reactive model. Alerts are triggered only after water levels breach predetermined 
markers or following visual confirmation of flooding, essentially announcing disasters that 
are already occurring rather than forecasting events that will materialize hours ahead. Third, 
current systems do not differentiate flood risk across micro-regions despite significant 
variations in elevation, drainage capacity, and river proximity within the district. Generic 
district-wide warnings fail to account for localized vulnerabilities, resulting in both alert 
fatigue in low-risk areas and inadequate urgency communication in high-risk zones.  
  
The Cyclone Ditwah catastrophe starkly illustrated these failures, resulting in over 15,000 
displaced residents, economic losses exceeding LKR 3 billion, and 23 fatalities in the 
Gampaha District alone. Post-disaster assessments revealed that 73% of affected households 
received no advance warning whatsoever, while those who did received alerts averaging only 
35 minutes insufficient even for basic evacuation preparations. This preventable tragedy 
underscores the critical need for predictive, hyper-local flood forecasting that provides 
actionable intelligence hours before flood onset, enabling life-saving protective measures.  
  
1.3 Scope and Limitations  
  
In-Scope:  
- Predictive modeling using Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks trained on 6-7 
years of historical rainfall and flood data  
- Real-time monitoring dashboard displaying weather conditions, flood risk levels, and 
interactive hazard maps  
- Automated SMS alert dissemination to registered residents in identified risk zones - 
Administrative interface for disaster management personnel to validate predictions and 
coordinate response  
- Community coordination platform facilitating post-disaster relief efforts  
  
Out of Scope:  
- Physical IoT sensor deployment or water level monitoring hardware installation  
- Real-time satellite imagery analysis and advanced GIS integration  
- Geographic expansion beyond Gampaha District (model is intentionally localized)  
- Prediction windows exceeding 12 hours (accuracy decreases beyond this threshold)  
- Comprehensive real-world validation across multiple monsoon seasons (requires ongoing 
operational deployment)

Chapter 02: Business Case  
  
2.1 Business Need  
  
The Cyclone Ditwah disaster provides quantifiable evidence of current system inadequacy. 
Preliminary damage assessments documented direct property losses exceeding LKR 3.2 
billion, including 4,200 homes destroyed or severely damaged and 1,850 hectares of 
agricultural land inundated. Historical records show major flooding in the Aththanagalu Oya 
basin occurred in 2016, 2017, 2018, 2020, 2022, and 2024, with cumulative economic impact 
over the past decade exceeding LKR 15 billion.  
  
Community interviews following Cyclone Ditwah revealed consistent stakeholder needs. 
Survey data indicated 94% of respondents would implement protective measures if receiving 
warnings 4-6 hours in advance, while 89% stated warnings under one hour provided 
insufficient preparation time. Small business operators indicated 3-4 hours advance notice 
would enable inventory protection and flood barrier implementation, potentially reducing 
losses by 50-70%.  
  
Recent advances in Long Short-Term Memory neural networks have demonstrated 85-95% 
accuracy for flood prediction in similar river basins, representing mature, implementable 
technology. International best practices show effective early warning systems can reduce 
disaster mortality by up to 90%, creating a compelling humanitarian imperative alongside 
economic justification.

 Chapter 04: Literature Review  
  
4.1 Search Strategy  
  
This review synthesizes research across hydrological flood prediction, deep learning for 
timeseries forecasting, and community-centered disaster risk reduction. Systematic searches 
employed IEEE Xplore, Google Scholar, ResearchGate, SpringerLink, ScienceDirect, and 
arXiv using keywords: "flood prediction," "LSTM neural network," "hydrological 
forecasting," "early warning systems," "disaster management Sri Lanka." Priority given to 
2018-2025 publications capturing recent deep learning advances.  
  
4.2 Deep Learning for Flood Prediction  
  
Hochreiter and Schmidhuber's (1997) foundational LSTM architecture addressed vanishing 
gradients in recurrent networks, enabling effective temporal sequence learning. This 
capability proves particularly relevant for flood prediction where precipitation patterns 
influence river discharge hours later.  
  
Recent research demonstrates LSTM superiority in flood forecasting. Kratzert et al. (2018) 
showed LSTM networks trained on multi-basin datasets achieved Nash-Sutcliffe efficiency 
scores exceeding 0.7 for 531 basins across the United States, suggesting models capture 
fundamental rainfall-runoff relationships transcending specific geographic contexts. Le et al.  
(2019) compared LSTM against Support Vector Machines, Random Forests, and 
AutoRegressive models in Vietnam's Da River basin, with LSTM achieving 15-23% higher 
accuracy and 40% reduction in mean absolute error, particularly excelling at capturing 
nonlinear relationships during storm events.  
  
However, Mosavi et al. (2018) reviewed 88 machine learning flood prediction studies, noting 
performance varies significantly based on data quality, temporal resolution, and spatially 
distributed input features. Studies using hourly rainfall consistently outperformed daily 
aggregates, while soil moisture and land-use data improved accuracy by 8-15%.  
  
4.3 Data Requirements and Feature Engineering  
  
Sit et al. (2020) analyzed 140 deep learning water resources applications, identifying 
common challenges including missing observations, inconsistent temporal resolution, and 
inadequate spatial monitoring coverage. Kao et al. (2021) demonstrated optimal LSTM 
performance requires time-series data spanning multiple hydrological cycles, with 5-7 years 
of historical data achieving stable performance while <3 years exhibited overfitting 
tendencies.  
  
Effective input features identified across studies include hourly rainfall intensity, cumulative 
rainfall over multiple windows, rate of rainfall change, antecedent soil moisture, distributed 
rainfall across upstream sub-catchments, elevation derivatives, land use classifications, and 
current water levels. Shen (2018) emphasized StandardScaler normalization outperformed 
min-max scaling for rainfall and water level data exhibiting outlier extremes during flood 
events.

4.4 Early Warning Systems and Alert Dissemination  
  
UNDRR and WMO (2022) framework for people-centered early warning systems identifies 
four essential elements: risk knowledge, monitoring and warning service, dissemination and 
communication, and response capability. Paul and Rahman (2023) evaluated Bangladesh's 
Flood Forecasting Centre SMS alert system serving 3 million subscribers, finding 6-12 hour 
warning lead times enabled 73% of households to implement protective measures compared 
to 28% for warnings received <2 hours before flooding. Message content significantly 
influenced response, with specific guidance generating 47% higher compliance than generic 
warnings.  
  
Meyer et al. (2019) analyzed public response across European river systems, finding false 
alarm rates exceeding 20% led to progressive trust erosion, with each false alarm reducing 
subsequent warning compliance by 7-12%. This underscores importance of conservative alert 
thresholds and clear uncertainty communication.  
  
4.5 Sri Lankan Context  
  
Chathurani et al. (2022) documented Aththanagalu Oya basin flood response time averages 
only 3-5 hours due to steep upper catchment slopes, short flow distances, and extensive 
impervious surfaces in urbanized areas. This rapid response creates particular early warning 
challenges as prediction errors compound quickly. Wickramagamage (2020) analyzed 30 
years rainfall data for Gampaha District, identifying extreme rainfall event frequency 
(>150mm in 24 hours) increased 18% between 2000-2019 compared to 1980-1999, aligning 
with climate change projections.  
  
Sri Lanka's national SMS alert system launched in 2023 serves approximately 1.2 million 
registered users nationwide but operates reactively rather than predictively, creating the gap 
FloodGuard addresses.  
  
4.6 Research Gaps  
  
Localized Application in Data-Constrained Contexts: Most research focuses on large river 
basins with extensive monitoring infrastructure. Aththanagalu Oya has limited monitoring 
with only 2-3 official rainfall stations. FloodGuard must demonstrate LSTM models achieve 
acceptable accuracy even with sparse ground-truth data.  
  
Integration of Prediction with Action: Literature extensively documents prediction 
methodologies but provides limited guidance on system integration, how predictions translate 
into actionable warnings, how uncertainty communicates to non-technical audiences, and 
how systems handle edge cases.  
  
Community Coordination Infrastructure: Existing research focuses almost exclusively on 
warning dissemination with minimal attention to post-event relief coordination. FloodGuard's 
community platform represents novel integration of predictive warning with response 
facilitation.

Chapter 05: Method of Approach  
  
5.1 Research Design and Project Management Methodology  
The development process is managed using an Agile, Scrum-based methodology, employing 
bi-weekly (two-week) sprints. Agile is particularly suitable for this project due to the 
exploratory nature of machine learning development and the potential for evolving 
requirements based on data quality, model performance, and stakeholder feedback. Iterative 
development enables early validation of system components and reduces the risk of late-stage 
design failure.  
Each sprint produces a measurable and reviewable increment of functionality, allowing 
continuous evaluation against project objectives. Regular sprint reviews with the supervisor 
support formative feedback and ensure alignment with academic and technical expectations.  
Indicative Sprint Structure:  
• Sprint 1: Requirements finalization, system architecture design, UI wireframes  
• Sprint 2: User authentication, role-based access control, core user profiles  
• Sprint 3: Flood prediction workflows, administrative dashboard, data model 
implementation  
• Sprint 4: Alert dissemination services, logging, and error handling  
• Sprint 5: Machine learning integration, dashboards, and validation views  
• Sprint 6: System testing, security hardening, performance optimization, and 
documentation  
  
5.2 Data Sources and Collection Strategy  
FloodGuard integrates historical and real-time data to support predictive modelling and 
operational alerting.  
Historical Flood Data is sourced from the Disaster Management Centre and the Department 
of Irrigation. These datasets include documented flood dates, affected locations, duration, and 
impact summaries covering approximately 25 - 40 flood events between 2018 and 2024. 
Official reports are cross-referenced with verified media archives to improve data 
completeness and reliability.  
Real-Time Weather Data is obtained via the OpenWeatherMap API, with AccuWeather 
configured as a fallback provider. Weather parameters include rainfall intensity, temperature, 
humidity, wind speed, atmospheric pressure, and cloud cover, updated at 10 - 15 minute 
intervals.

Data Governance and Ethics:  
All user-related data, including phone numbers and location information, are encrypted and 
access-controlled. Explicit user consent is obtained during registration, and the system design 
complies with Sri Lanka’s Personal Data Protection Act. Data used for evaluation and 
reporting purposes is anonymized.  
  
5.3 Technology Stack and Implementation Tools  
  
• Machine Learning: TensorFlow 2.x, Keras, Pandas, NumPy, Scikit-learn  
• Backend: Node.js, Express.js, MongoDB, Mongoose, JWT, SMS Gateway  
• Frontend: React.js, Tailwind CSS, Leaflet.js, Chart.js, Recharts  
• Deployment: Vercel, Netlify, Heroku, MongoDB Atlas, GitHub Actions  
  
  
5.4 Machine Learning Model Design  
Flood prediction is implemented using a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neural network, 
selected for its effectiveness in modelling temporal dependencies in sequential data.  
Input features include rainfall intensity, cumulative rainfall windows, rate of change, 
temperature, humidity, soil moisture proxies, and seasonal indicators.  
Training configuration:  
• Batch size: 32  
• Epochs: 50 - 100 with early stopping  
• Validation split: 20%  
• Optimizer: Adam (learning rate 0.001)  
• Class weighting to address imbalance
