1. Problem Statement 
 
Flooding is one of the most frequent natural disasters in Sri Lanka, especially  in low-lying 
areas like the Gampaha District. This is a consequence of two main factors: intense 
monsoonal rainfall and accelerated urbanization., unplanned construction, and insufficient 
drainage infrastructure, the Aththanagalu Oya river basin, which flows through densely 
populated areas like Ja-Ela, Gampaha, and Minuwangoda, is extremely vulnerable to sudden 
floods. Even though these floods occur frequently, today's early warning systems are still 
insufficient and poorly coordinated. They are designed to react to dangers as they happen, 
rather than proactively warning people before an event occurs. 
 
In Sri Lanka, traditional flood alert systems frequently rely on centralized announcements 
from the Department of Irrigation, fixed thresholds, or human monitoring. These approaches 
are not area-specific enough slow response times, and restricted community-level 
distribution, particularly during periods of high rainfall when prompt decision-making is 
crucial. Because of this, residents often receive warnings too late to take appropriate action, 
which results in preventable property damage, displacement, and in extreme cases, fatalities. 
 
Although more dynamic flood forecasting models have been made possible by global 
advancements in artificial intelligence and remote sensing, their integration into localized 
disaster management in Sri Lanka is still very limited. Additionally Many current systems use 
general models that cannot handle the complex water flow of a specific river, like the 
Aththanagalu Oya. Hence, they don't fully use past data on rainfall, river levels, and ground 
conditions, which is needed to make very accurate predictions. 
 
A flood prediction and early warning system designed especially for the Aththanagalu Oya 
basin is desperately needed in this situation. The system should be able to predict short-term 
flood risk with a reasonable level of accuracy by utilizing deep learning techniques such as 
LSTM,  RNN models, historical flood datasets, and near real-time weather/rainfall inputs. In 
order to guarantee that locals receive timely and understandable guidance, the platform must 
also give priority to actionable community-level alerts via accessible channels like SMS, 
WhatsApp, or mobile notifications. 
 
The primary objective is to transition from a reactive approach to disaster response towards 
proactive risk management. An intelligent, localized early warning system will enable both 
authorities and residents to make informed decisions, reduce damage, and safeguard 
vulnerable communities in the Gampaha District from the increasing risk of flooding.

2. Project Description  
 
2.1 Background of the Project 
 
The Gampaha district in Sri Lanka is known to be a high-risk area for flooding. The 
Aththanagalu Oya river basin, in particular, faces this problem repeatedly. Research has 
confirmed why this area is vulnerable since it is very flat and low-lying, it is densely 
populated with homes and farms, and the main waterways that should drain into the 
Negombo Lagoon are often blocked (Chathurani et al., 2022). 
 
Recently, Sri Lanka launched a national system to send disaster alerts to citizens via SMS 
(climatefactchecks.org, 2025). While this system is good for delivering messages, it has a 
critical gap: it cannot predict a flood at the local level before it happens. This means residents 
in the Aththanagalu Oya basin often get warnings too late, just as the water is already rising. 
This project will fill that prediction gap. We will use a modern and effective method known 
as deep learning, specifically a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network. This type of AI 
model has been proven to be very effective for predicting floods in real-time, even in 
complex river systems (Moutaouakil et al., 2025). 
 
These new, early predictions can then be fed directly into Sri Lanka’s existing national SMS 
alert system. This connects our local forecast with the national warning channel, allowing the 
Disaster Management Centre to send life-saving alerts before the flood becomes dangerous 
(climatefactchecks.org, 2025). This project bridges a critical gap, empowers the Gampaha 
community, and creates a system that could eventually be used to protect other flood-prone 
areas in the country. 
 
 
2.2 Project Objectives 
 
• Develop a localized flood risk prediction and alert platform 
 
Design and implement a web based platform exclusive to Gampaha District, where citizens 
and government authorities can monitor flood hazards and be given early warnings. 
To train its predictive model, the system will collect and prepare environmental data such like 
rainfall and river levels from both past records and real-time sources. The platform will 
provide neighborhood based flood risk levels, interactive maps, and historic flood patterns for 
awareness depending on these inputs. 
 
• Implement a deep learning based forecasting model 
 
The system's predictive engine will be built on a Long Short -Term Memory (LSTM) neural 
network, rather than a general statistical model. This model will be constructed to predict short-
term future flood risk based on temporal trends in rainfall and water level rise, with spatial 
adjustments made using land elevation and soil absorption data. 
If anomalies or critical flood thresholds are detected, the model will trigger alerts to the front-
end and connected communication modules.

• Send proactive SMS-based flood alerts and guidance 
 
The system will have an SMS integration layer to ensure timely delivery of warning warnings 
to inhabitants, especially those in regions with no internet. FloodGuard will deliver district 
level alerts, safety advice, and evacuation instructions automatically through local SMS APIs.  
 
• Enable real-time coordination for volunteers and donations 
 
A dedicated community section will allow local government, NGOs, and volunteers to 
coordinate disaster response efforts. 
Features include: 
• Donation tracking and resource request listings 
• Volunteer registration and availability scheduling 
• Dashboard for administrators to post verified updates 
• A community feed for affected residents to seek help or report damage 
Each listing will include reaction metrics such as volunteer count, and verified status to build 
trust and transparency. 
This module draws inspiration from successful disaster relief initiatives and seeks to digitally 
amplify Sri Lanka’s strong culture of local solidarity in the face of crises. 
 
2.3 Scope of the Project 
 
Features and Functionality Covered in This Project 
• Forecasting floods using a deep learning-based LSTM model, trained on 6 - 7 years of 
rainfall and water level data from the Aththanagalu Oya and surrounding Gampaha 
region. 
• A web-based dashboard displaying current weather, flood predictions, rainfall trends 
for public access and authority monitoring. 
• Automated early warning system via SMS alerts using integration with local telecom 
APIs. 
• Admin panel for disaster response teams to: 
▪ Send alerts manually during emergencies. 
▪ Update risk zones. 
▪ View system usage statistics. 
• Data preprocessing and normalization pipelines for cleaning and formatting 
meteorological and hydrological datasets. Community reporting features, where 
registered NGOs or residents can share support/donation needs after a flood event. 
 
Out of Scope 
• Real-time IoT sensor integration. 
• Integration with satellite imaging or GIS platforms. 
• Full-scale nationwide flood prediction.

3. Research Gap 
 
 
While Sri Lanka has national disaster warning systems, many existing solutions have key 
limitations. They are often too general. This leaves vulnerable communities in flood-prone 
areas like Gampaha without the timely alerts they need. 
 
This project identifies and addresses three specific gaps in the current approach: 
 
Technological Gap 
Many current flood alert systems rely on simple alert triggers (e.g., sounding an alarm only 
after a river reaches a certain height) or manual human observation. These systems lack the 
power to predict a flood before it happens. Advanced methods like deep learning, particularly 
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, are scientifically proven to be highly effective 
for time-series forecasting (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). However, this predictive 
technology is not widely used in Sri Lanka's regional flood management. 
 
Contextual Gap 
Current flood models are often not customized for the local risks of a specific area like the 
Aththanagalu Oya basin. Much of the existing research in deep learning is applied to very 
large, global rivers or major "smart cities" (Goodfellow et al., 2016), while smaller, high-risk 
river basins are overlooked. Furthermore, crucial local data such as specific land-use patterns, 
soil types, or mining activities that directly impact how an area floods is often not included in 
the prediction models. 
 
Integration Gap 
Currently, disaster response tools often work in isolation an alert system is separate from a 
flood map, which is separate from a community support dashboard. There is a need for 
unified platform that integrates all these elements. Combining real-time predictions, alerts, 
and community coordination into one system is a modern approach to managing complex 
challenges (Siemens, 2013). This project aims to close this gap, moving from a disconnected 
approach to a centralized, integrated system. 
 
 
 
This project addresses these gaps by: 
• Developing a predictive model using deep learning (LSTM) for rainfall and river 
levels. 
• Focusing this model on the specific context of a vulnerable area (Gampaha) using 
local datasets. 
• Integrating the predictive model with accessible SMS alerts to ensure inclusive reach. 
• Building a single, centralized platform that combines all these features.
