Chapter 01: Introduction 
1.1 Introduction 
Flooding is one of the most frequent and damaging natural hazards in Sri Lanka, and its 
effects are felt most severely in low-lying, densely populated districts where rivers respond 
rapidly to heavy rainfall. The Gampaha District is a clear example of this vulnerability: rapid 
urbanisation, unplanned construction, and limited drainage capacity increase surface runoff, 
while the Aththanagalu Oya river basin flowing through areas such as Ja-Ela, Gampaha, and 
Minuwangoda creates conditions for sudden and fast-rising flood events that can overwhelm 
communities within hours. 
Recent disasters have highlighted how serious this situation has become. In late 2025, 
Cyclone ‘Ditwah’ brought unusually intense rainfall to southwestern Sri Lanka, and 
Gampaha experienced water levels rising by over two meters within a short period, leading to 
widespread inundation. The impacts were not only physical damage; they were personal and 
immediate families forced to evacuate at night, roads cut off, essential services disrupted, and 
thousands moved into temporary shelters. Official summaries documented 23 confirmed 
fatalities, the displacement of over 15,000 residents, and direct property losses exceeding 
LKR 3.2 billion. Even more concerning, post-disaster findings reported that about 73% of 
affected households received no advance warning, and those who did received alerts with an 
average lead time of only around 35 minutes, which is not enough for meaningful preparation 
or safe evacuation. 
These patterns are not isolated. Major flood events have repeatedly occurred across 2016, 
2017, 2018, 2020, 2022, and 2024, creating cumulative long-term economic and social 
damage and steadily weakening community resilience. The Aththanagalu Oya catchment is 
particularly challenging because it can move from heavy rainfall to peak flooding very 
quickly, meaning warning systems must provide earlier lead time than what current reactive 
approaches can offer. 
To address this gap, FloodGuard is proposed as a deep learning based flood prediction and 
early warning platform tailored specifically for the Aththanagalu Oya basin and the wider 
Gampaha District. The system is designed to shift flood management from a reactive model 
where alerts are triggered only when danger is already present into a proactive, data-driven 
approach. FloodGuard integrates (i) an LSTM-based prediction engine trained on historical 
rainfall and flood patterns, (ii) a real-time monitoring dashboard for citizens and authorities, 
and (iii) SMS-based alert dissemination to reach residents quickly, including those with 
limited internet access. The overall goal is to deliver actionable 6 - 12 hour flood-risk 
forecasts, giving families and response teams enough time to protect property, organise 
evacuations, and reduce preventable loss.

1.2 Problem Definition 
The core problem FloodGuard addresses is not merely the occurrence of floods, but the 
systemic inadequacy of how communities are warned and how institutions respond. Three 
interconnected failures define this problem. 
First, existing early warning systems in Sri Lanka provide critically insufficient lead time. 
The national disaster warning infrastructure, while operational, functions reactively triggering 
alerts only after river levels breach fixed thresholds or after visual confirmation of flooding 
by human observers. This means the current 30 to 45 minute alert window is effectively 
equivalent to no warning at all, since effective evacuation and asset protection require a 
minimum of 3 to 6 hours (Paul & Rahman, 2023). The Aththanagalu Oya basin compounds 
this challenge further because it has a flash response profile  the interval between significant 
rainfall onset and peak river discharge is only 3 to 5 hours, leaving essentially no margin in 
the existing system (Chathurani et al., 2022). 
Second, existing systems are geographically generic. Current alerts issued by the Disaster 
Management Centre of Sri Lanka cover the district rather than differentiating between high-
risk micro-zones such as riverside low-lying settlements and comparatively safer elevated 
areas. This lack of geographic granularity produces alert fatigue in lower-risk populations 
while potentially under-communicating urgency to those most at risk. 
Third, tools for disaster response flood mapping, prediction dashboards, community 
coordination platforms, and SMS alert systems currently exist in isolation from one another. 
There is no single, integrated platform where a citizen can check the current flood risk, an 
administrator can review model predictions and dispatch an alert, and a community member 
can request support or track donations. This fragmentation degrades overall response 
effectiveness. 
FloodGuard is designed to resolve all three of these failures through a unified, predictive, and 
community-oriented platform. 
1.3 Project Objectives 
• To develop a localised, deep learning-based flood prediction model using Long Short-
Term Memory (LSTM) neural networks, trained on historical rainfall and river level 
data specific to the Aththanagalu Oya basin, capable of generating flood risk forecasts 
with a minimum 6-hour lead time. 
• To build a comprehensive data pipeline that ingests, validates, and processes 
meteorological and hydrological datasets from multiple sources, including CSV batch 
imports and real-time API feeds. 
• To design and implement a responsive, user-facing web application providing live 
flood risk indicators, an interactive Leaflet.js map of the Gampaha region, current 
weather conditions, and historical prediction data. 
• To create an administrative dashboard enabling disaster management personnel to 
monitor system predictions, manage user accounts, approve and dispatch flood alerts, 
and review analytics. 
• To integrate an SMS-based alert dissemination mechanism that delivers timely 
warnings to registered users based on their flood zone, ensuring that residents without 
internet access receive critical information.

Chapter 02: System Analysis 
2.1 Facts Gathering Techniques 
The system analysis phase of FloodGuard drew on a combination of academic literature 
review, domain data analysis, community-level survey and informal interviews, and a 
structured evaluation of comparable existing systems. This multi-source approach ensured 
that the system requirements were grounded in both scientific evidence and real user needs in 
flood-prone communities within the Gampaha District. 
❖ Literature and Domain Research: The foundational understanding of flood risk in 
the Gampaha District was built through a review of published academic studies. 
Chathurani et al. (2022) provided detailed hydrological and geographical analysis of 
the Aththanagalu Oya basin, documenting its flat terrain, high population density, and 
poor drainage as key vulnerability factors. Wickramagamage (2020) contributed 
rainfall trend data showing an 18% increase in extreme rainfall events in the Gampaha 
District between 2000 and 2019 compared to the preceding two decades, establishing 
that the problem is worsening over time. Research by Moutaouakil et al. (2025) and 
Le et al. (2019) provided empirical evidence that LSTM-based models can deliver 
reliable, real-time flood predictions even in complex watersheds. 
❖ Environmental Data Analysis: Historical rainfall records, river gauge readings, and 
flood occurrence data were reviewed from sources including the Disaster 
Management Centre of Sri Lanka and the Department of Irrigation. These reviews 
revealed significant limitations in the existing data infrastructure only 2 to 3 official 
rainfall monitoring stations exist within the Aththanagalu Oya catchment and 
established the requirement for a data generation and augmentation strategy to support 
model training. In response, two years of simulated monsoon-aware weather and river 
level datasets were generated for the Aththanagalu Oya, covering known seasonal 
patterns and extreme event profiles. 
❖ Community Survey for Local Opinions: 
To ensure FloodGuard aligns with real-world user expectations, a community survey 
was conducted among residents in flood-prone areas of the Gampaha District 
(including locations connected to Aththanagalu Oya and surrounding flood-risk 
zones).  
The survey was designed to capture: 
• common issues faced with current flood warnings, 
• preferred alert channels, 
• demand for location-specific predictions, and 
• willingness to adopt a predictive flood warning system.

2.2 Existing Systems 
1. Sri Lanka National SMS Disaster Alert System (2023) Sri Lanka's national SMS alert 
system, launched in 2023, serves approximately 1.2 million registered users nationwide and 
is capable of broadcasting warnings via mobile networks (Climate Fact Checks, 2023). While 
the system provides a valuable distribution channel, it operates entirely reactively, alerts are 
issued only after a disaster event has been confirmed or thresholds breached. It has no 
predictive capability, no local flood-risk modelling, and no integration with hydrological 
monitoring. 
2. Disaster Management Centre (DMC) Sri Lanka Portal The DMC portal provides 
publicly accessible situational updates during disasters. It serves as a centralised 
communication point for government agencies but is not designed for citizen-facing real-time 
risk monitoring. Information is manually updated, lacks geospatial granularity, and does not 
include any flood prediction functionality. 
3. FEMA Mobile Application (United States) The FEMA app (Bachmann et al., 2015) 
represents a mature example of a multi-feature disaster management tool, including safety 
tips, shelter locators, and real-time National Weather Service alerts. However, it operates 
within a far more resource-intensive infrastructure than what is feasible for the Gampaha 
context, and it does not incorporate predictive flood modelling.

2.3 Drawbacks of Existing Systems 
Lack of Predictive Capability: None of the systems currently accessible to Gampaha 
residents offer predictive flood risk modelling. They all operate reactively, issuing warnings 
only after dangerous conditions have already developed. This leaves communities with no 
time for meaningful protective action (UNDRR & WMO, 2022). 
Geographic Generalization: Existing systems issue district-wide or national-level alerts, 
failing to differentiate risk across micro-zones within a single district. Communities in lower-
risk areas receive the same alerts as those at the highest risk, contributing to alert fatigue and 
reducing overall compliance with warnings (Meyer et al., 2019). 
Fragmented Tools: Currently, flood prediction, alert dissemination, administrative 
management, and community coordination exist as entirely separate systems that do not 
communicate with one another. This fragmentation slows response times and creates 
information gaps during rapidly evolving flood events. 
Limited Accessibility: Several existing platforms require internet connectivity and modern 
smartphones. Given that a significant proportion of at-risk residents in Gampaha are older, 
less technically literate, or in areas with poor connectivity, any system that cannot reach users 
via SMS is fundamentally incomplete. 
No Community Coordination Layer: None of the systems reviewed provide features for 
post-disaster community coordination such as volunteer registration

3.4 Networking Requirements 
The system is deployed as a three-container Docker Compose stack. The backend Express 
API communicates with the MongoDB container over an internal Docker bridge network on 
port 27017. The React frontend, served by Nginx, communicates with the backend via HTTP 
on port 5000. External users access the frontend on port 5173. SMS alert requests are 
dispatched from the backend to the external SMS gateway over HTTPS. All sensitive routes 
are protected by JWT middleware and are not directly accessible to the frontend without a 
valid token. 
Chapter 04: Feasibility Study 
4.1 Operational Feasibility 
FloodGuard is designed with practical, community-level deployment in mind. The public-
facing interface requires only a web browser and an internet connection or, for SMS-based 
alerts, only a basic mobile phone. This low barrier to access is critical in the Gampaha 
context, where digital literacy varies widely across age groups and the urban-rural divide. 
Administrative users most likely personnel from local government offices or the Disaster 
Management Centre interact with a structured dashboard that presents information clearly 
and requires no specialised technical training. The admin panel follows familiar web interface 
conventions: paginated tables, form inputs for manual data entry, and clear labels for risk 
levels. 
The data pipeline's CSV batch import capability ensures that the system can be populated 
rapidly with historical records, even if data is initially sourced from non-digital formats such 
as printed reports or spreadsheets. This pragmatic design reduces the dependency on real-
time API access during initial deployment phases. 
Operationally, the primary challenge lies in user registration building a base of registered 
users large enough to make the SMS alert system impactful. This will require community 
outreach, NGO partnerships, and coordination with local government divisions. These are 
social and administrative challenges rather than technical ones, and they are addressed in the 
project's outreach planning. 
4.2 Economical Feasibility 
FloodGuard is built exclusively on open-source technologies, with no commercial licensing 
costs. The technology stack Node.js, Express, MongoDB, React, Python, TensorFlow is 
freely available. Docker and Docker Compose are similarly free for non-enterprise use. 
The primary cost drivers for deployment are cloud hosting and SMS gateway usage. 
Estimated monthly hosting costs for a small to medium-scale deployment using a standard 
cloud VPS are in the range of LKR 5,000 to 10,000. SMS alert costs depend on volume; at 
LKR 2 to 3 per message, a database of 1,000 registered users receiving one alert during a 
flood event costs approximately LKR 2,000 to 3,000 per alert cycle. These costs are well 
within the budget parameters established in the project proposal (total estimated project cost:

The economic case for the system's value is also clear: post-disaster property losses in the

indicates that even modest warning improvements can reduce household property losses by 
30 to 50% by enabling owners to protect moveable assets before floodwaters arrive (UNDRR 
& WMO, 2022). 
 
4.3 Technical Feasibility 
The technical approach is grounded in well-established, proven technologies. LSTM neural 
networks are the state-of-the-art method for time-series hydrological forecasting, with a 
substantial body of peer-reviewed evidence supporting their effectiveness in exactly this type 
of application. Kratzert et al. (2018) demonstrated LSTM models achieving Nash-Sutcliffe 
efficiency scores exceeding 0.7 across 531 river basins, confirming their generalisation 
capability. More recent work by Kim et al. (2025) confirms that LSTM models produce high-
accuracy flood level predictions within 3-hour lead times even in river systems with limited 
monitoring infrastructure directly relevant to the Aththanagalu Oya context. 
The web application stack (React + Vite, Node.js + Express, MongoDB) is mature, widely 
documented, and the developer has demonstrated proficiency through the substantial 
implementation progress already achieved. Docker-based containerisation has been 
successfully implemented and tested. The data pipeline, including CSV import, automatic 
threshold computation, and database seeding, is operational. The key remaining technical 
challenge is the training and integration of the LSTM model itself, which is scheduled for the 
current development sprint.

6.1 Development Methodology 
FloodGuard is developed using the Agile Development methodology with a Scrum-inspired 
approach, because the system is feature-based and can be delivered incrementally as 
independent modules (Satria, Rahma & Muftikhali, 2024). This is especially suitable for 
FloodGuard because the machine learning component (LSTM flood prediction) requires 
iterative experimentation with datasets, model tuning, and integration testing, while the web 
platform modules (data pipeline, dashboards, authentication, and alerts) can be developed and 
validated in parallel. 
The project follows two-week sprint cycles, where each sprint targets a clear deliverable such 
as a backend API feature, a frontend module, a dataset ingestion function, or a deployable 
integration improvement. At the end of each sprint, the work is reviewed through functional 
testing and demonstration of implemented features. Supervisor meetings act as informal 
sprint reviews, where feedback is used to update priorities and refine the upcoming sprint 
backlog. Stakeholder input is also planned as part of the project, particularly from local 
disaster-response organisations (e.g., the Disaster Management Centre and relevant 
community-level actors), to validate alert usability and operational relevance once the 
prediction and SMS modules are activated. 
Based on the current scope and progress, the development is organized into the following 
phases: 
Phase 1: Planning and Requirements Analysis (Completed) 
• Define Project scope and objectives  
• Initial research on data sources and APIs 
• Key Milestone : Project Proposal Approval and Submission 
Phase 2: System Design, Architecture and wireframing (Completed) 
• Design system Architecture  
• Creating UI/UX wireframes 
• Key Milestone - Project Initiation Document (PID) submission 
Phase 3: Core Implementation (Frontend + Backend)  (Substantially Completed) 
• Implement authentication (public + admin) and JWT protection 
• Implement data pipeline (CSV upload + manual entry + status calculations) 
• Implement prediction APIs with seeded/stub data 
• Build public dashboard pages + admin dashboard + user management 
• Docker Compose deployment and networking verified 
• Key Milestone: Core platform operational with working UI + APIs

6.2 Programming Languages and Tools 
1. JavaScript (Node.js / React) 
• The primary implementation language across both the frontend and backend. 
JavaScript's single-language full-stack capability reduces context-switching overhead 
and enables code sharing between tiers where appropriate. 
 
2. Python (TensorFlow 2.x / Keras):  
• The ML model training environment. Python was selected for its unmatched 
ecosystem for machine learning, with TensorFlow and Keras providing the LSTM 
model architecture, training pipeline, and inference interface. 
3. MongoDB Query Language (MQL):  
• Used for constructing all database queries. MongoDB's document model aligns well 
with the variable-structure nature of meteorological and hydrological data. 
4. Docker / Docker Compose:  
• Containerisation of all three application tiers. Ensures consistent, reproducible 
deployment and eliminates environment-specific bugs during development and 
testing. 
5. Vite: 
• The frontend build tool. Provides fast hot-module replacement during development 
and optimised production bundles. 
6. Postman:  
• Used throughout the backend development phase to test and document all API 
endpoints before frontend integration. 
 
7. GitHub:  
• Version control and collaborative development. All commits are descriptively 
labelled to maintain a clear development history.

6.4 Algorithms 
Flood Risk Classification Algorithm: Upon insertion of a new Prediction document, the 
system applies a rule-based classification algorithm to assign a risk level based on flood 
probability. Probabilities above 80% are classified as Critical; 60 - 79% as High; 40 - 59% as 
Moderate; 20 - 39% as Low; and below 20% as None. This classification drives the colour-
coded risk display on the public predictions page. 
River Status Threshold Algorithm: When river level data is ingested either via CSV or 
manual entry - the system automatically computes the status field by comparing the measured 
level against the configurable normalThreshold, alertThreshold, and criticalThreshold stored 
per monitoring location. This eliminates the need for manual status entry and ensures 
consistency across all ingested records. 
LSTM Flood Prediction Model (In Development): The core predictive algorithm uses a 
Long Short-Term Memory neural network trained on sequences of hourly rainfall, 
cumulative rainfall windows, river level readings, temperature, humidity, and seasonal 
indicators. The model architecture comprises an input LSTM layer, a dropout regularisation 
layer, a second LSTM layer, and a dense output layer producing flood probability as a 
continuous value between 0 and 1. Training uses the Adam optimiser with a learning rate of 
0.001, binary cross entropy loss (for the classification framing), and early stopping to prevent 
overfitting. The model targets accuracy ≥ 90%, precision ≥ 85%, and recall ≥ 90% on the 
held-out validation split covering the 2023 - 2024 period (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; 
Le et al., 2019).

Chapter 07: Implementation Progress 
7.1 Development Environment Setup 
The FloodGuard development environment is orchestrated using Docker Compose, which 
containerizes the full three-tier stack to ensure consistent and reproducible deployment across 
different machines. The stack consists of three interconnected services: a MongoDB database 
container (persisted using the named Docker volume floodguard_data), a Node.js/Express 
(Express 5) backend API container running on port 5000, and a React (Vite) frontend that is 
built in production mode and served through Nginx (container port 80, exposed on host port 
5173). All services communicate through an internal Docker bridge network (floodguard-
network), allowing reliable service-to-service addressing using Docker service names (e.g., 
mongodb://mongo:27017/floodguard) rather than localhost. 
 
The frontend is developed using React 19 with React Router for SPA navigation, and in the 
Docker production build it is delivered via Nginx using SPA routing rules Nginx also acts as 
a reverse proxy for API traffic by forwarding all API requests to the backend service 
(http://backend:5000), meaning the browser interacts with a single origin while the backend 
remains isolated within the internal network. 
 
The backend runs on Node.js and follows a modular structure with clearly separated routes, 
controllers, models, middleware, and scripts. It loads environment variables using dotenv, 
connects to MongoDB via Mongoose, and applies JWT-based protection for administrator-
only routes. Seed scripts and sample CSV datasets are maintained to initialise admin accounts 
and populate stub prediction and hydrological records for testing. 
 
For local development without containers, MongoDB can be started separately (locally or via 
Atlas), then the backend can be run using npm run dev (nodemon on port 5000) and the 
frontend using npm run dev (Vite HMR on port 5173), with configuration provided via a .env 
file in the backend. The full codebase is version-controlled in GitHub with a consistent 
commit history to track sprint-level progress and feature delivery. 
7.2 Implemented Features 
Database Models - Fully Implemented: All five primary MongoDB schemas are defined 
and operational: Admin, User, Prediction, WeatherData, and RiverLevel. Each model 
includes field-level validation, default values, and timestamp tracking. 
User Authentication - Fully Implemented: Public users can register via POST 
/api/users/register and log in via POST /api/users/login. Administrator authentication via 
POST /api/admin/login returns a signed JWT used to protect all subsequent admin-facing 
operations. A one-time admin setup endpoint and a seed script (seedAdmin.js) establish the 
default admin account on first deployment. 
Admin User Management - Fully Implemented: The admin API exposes full CRUD 
operations on public user accounts, with server-side pagination, keyword search, and filtering 
by flood zone and account status. The admin dashboard displays real-time user statistics 
including total users, active users, and a list of distinct flood zones.

Admin Login, Admin Dashboard with user statistics, and User Management with search, 
filter, and pagination.

7.4 Challenges Encountered and Solutions 
Challenge 1 - Data Scarcity: Obtaining official hydrological data from the Department of 
Irrigation proved more time-consuming than anticipated, as formal data-sharing agreements 
require institutional approval processes. The interim solution was to generate two years of 
synthetic monsoon-aware data using known climatic patterns for the Aththanagalu Oya 
region, supplemented with Kaggle-sourced flood datasets. This approach, while not a 
replacement for authoritative sensor data, is consistent with strategies documented in the 
literature for training ML models in data-constrained environments (Sit et al., 2020). 
Challenge 2 - JWT Token Expiry Handling: During early testing of the admin dashboard, 
expired tokens were causing silent failures on API calls rather than explicit re-authentication 
prompts. This was resolved by implementing Axios interceptors on the frontend that detect 
401 responses and redirect the user to the login page with a clear expiry message. 
Challenge 3 - CSV Streaming Performance: Initial CSV import attempts for the full two-
year weather dataset caused request timeouts due to synchronous row-by-row processing. 
Refactoring the import handler to use a streaming parser with batched database inserts 
(inserting every 500 rows) resolved the timeout issue and reduced import time significantly. 
Challenge 4 - Docker Network Configuration: Early Docker Compose configurations used 
localhost references within container environment variables, which failed because containers 
communicate via service names, not localhost. Updating all internal connection strings to use 
Docker service names (e.g., mongodb://mongo:27017/floodguard) resolved cross-container 
connectivity. 
Challenge 4 - Role separated authentication flow complexity 
Challenge: Admin and regular users require different access models. 
Solution: Distinct route namespaces and separate controller flows (/api/admin vs /api/users). 
 
7.5 Current System Limitations 
ML Model Not Yet Integrated: The most significant current limitation is that the prediction 
data displayed on the frontend is seeded dummy data rather than live LSTM model output. 
The model architecture is designed and the training pipeline is being prepared; integration is 
the primary focus of the current development sprint. 
SMS Alert System Not Yet Active: The SMS alert dissemination module has been 
architecturally designed and the relevant API endpoints are in place, but active integration 
with a live SMS gateway (Twilio or Dialog Axiata API) is pending. This will be completed 
alongside the ML model integration phase. 
Single Location Coverage: The current prediction seeding covers multiple location strings, 
but the LSTM model will initially be trained and calibrated for a single primary monitoring 
location  the Aththanagalu Oya at Ja-Ela. Multi-location coverage will be expanded in 
subsequent development iterations.

Chapter 08: Discussion 
Summary of the Report 
This interim report documents the current state of FloodGuard - a deep learning-based flood 
prediction and early warning system designed specifically for the Aththanagalu Oya basin 
and the Gampaha District of Sri Lanka. The system is motivated by a well-documented and 
serious public safety problem: the absence of any predictive, localised, and accessible flood 
warning mechanism in a region that experiences recurring, devastating flood events. 
The report has demonstrated that FloodGuard's core technical infrastructure is fully 
operational. The three-container Dockerized deployment environment is running. The 
database models are defined and seeded. The backend API - including user management, 
prediction data retrieval, and the full data pipeline for weather and river level ingestion is 
complete and tested. The React frontend serves live data to users across all core public and 
admin pages. Two years of synthetic hydrological data have been generated and are ready for 
LSTM model training. 
The primary remaining milestone is the training and integration of the LSTM flood prediction 
model itself, followed by activation of the SMS alert module. These two components 
represent the highest-value features of the system, the ones that will transform FloodGuard 
from a data management platform into a genuine early warning system. 
What Has Changed from the Proposal 
The foundational architecture described in the proposal remains intact. The major adaptation 
has been in the data sourcing strategy: the proposal anticipated direct data access from the 
Department of Irrigation, which has not yet been secured. In response, synthetic monsoon-
aware datasets have been generated as an interim measure, and Kaggle flood datasets are 
being used to prototype the ML training pipeline. These are clearly documented limitations, 
not design failures, and they do not compromise the integrity of the system architecture. 
The proposal also referenced MongoDB Atlas (cloud) as the database hosting solution. In 
practice, the development deployment uses a locally containerised MongoDB instance, which 
is more appropriate for the current development phase and avoids cloud hosting costs during 
iteration. Atlas remains the intended production deployment target. 
Future Plans and Upcoming Work 
The immediate next steps, in priority order, are: training the LSTM model on the available 
synthetic and Kaggle datasets and integrating its output into the API endpoint; activating the 
SMS gateway integration to enable live alert dispatch; implementing the automated 
OpenWeatherMap polling job to feed real-time weather data into the pipeline without manual
